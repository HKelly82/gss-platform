Agile in an assurance world

Agile and government assurance are often framed as opposites. They are not — but holding both at once is the real work of a delivery manager in the public sector.

When teams first move into government delivery, they tend to expect one of two things: that agile will be quietly ignored in favour of a fixed plan, or that “being agile” means assurance no longer applies. Both expectations cause problems within the first few sprints.

The Service Standard does not ask you to choose. It asks teams to work in an agile way — to start small, test with real users, and iterate — while remaining accountable for public money through spending controls and service assessments. The skill is not picking a side. It is making the two reinforce each other.

What assurance is actually asking for

An assurance gate is not a demand for a finished product. It is a checkpoint that asks a narrower question: is this team learning the right things, fast enough, to justify continued spend? Framed that way, frequent delivery is the strongest evidence you can bring.

Key principle
Treat each assurance point as a user need of its own: the assessor needs evidence the service is being built well. Plan to meet it, don’t scramble for it.

In practice this changes how you run a team. You keep a continuous trail of what you tested, what you learned, and what you changed — not as a separate reporting burden, but as the natural output of working in the open. By the time an assessment arrives, the narrative already exists.

Where delivery managers add the most

Three habits separate teams that thrive under assurance from teams that stall:

  • They make the team’s progress visible by default, so assurance never requires a special effort.
  • They translate between delivery language and governance language without distorting either.
  • They protect the team’s pace by handling assurance as a stream of work, not a series of fire drills.
Note
Spending controls apply above set thresholds and at specific points in a service’s life. The exact figures change — the reference card for this module keeps the current ones to hand.

Held together well, agile and assurance describe the same thing from two angles: a team that knows what it is building, why, and for whom — and can show it at any moment.
